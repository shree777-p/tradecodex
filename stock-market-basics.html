Stock Market Basics

Before any chart, indicator, or strategy makes sense, a handful of basic terms need to be second nature: what a share is, how its price is quoted, and what actually happens when that price moves too far, too fast.

What a share represents

A share is a unit of ownership in a company. Owning one share of a company with 10 crore shares outstanding means owning one ten-crore-th of that company — a proportional claim on its profits (via dividends, if declared) and, in principle, a vote on major company decisions. Share prices move based on how the market's collective expectation of the company's future changes, not on the company's past performance alone.

Market capitalization

Market capitalization ("market cap") is a company's share price multiplied by its total number of outstanding shares — a rough proxy for the company's total market value. Companies are commonly grouped by market cap into large-cap, mid-cap, and small-cap, a distinction that matters because it correlates with liquidity, volatility, and how much analyst coverage a stock typically gets.

Primary market vs secondary market

The primary market is where a company sells shares to the public for the first time — an IPO — receiving that money directly. The secondary market is what people usually mean by "the stock market": the day-to-day buying and selling of already-issued shares between investors, where the company itself isn't a party to the transaction and doesn't receive the proceeds.

Bid, ask, and the order book

At any moment, a stock has a bid (the highest price a buyer is currently offering) and an ask (the lowest price a seller is currently willing to accept). A trade executes when a buyer accepts the current ask or a seller accepts the current bid. The full list of pending buy and sell orders at every price level is the order book — covered in depth in Order Types.

Circuit limits

Indian exchanges apply circuit limits — a maximum percentage a stock is allowed to move in a single session, commonly set at 2%, 5%, 10%, or 20% depending on the stock. Hitting the upper circuit means the stock can't trade higher that day; hitting the lower circuit means it can't trade lower. Circuits exist to slow down panic-driven or manipulation-driven price spirals and give the market time to absorb new information.

Bull markets and bear markets

A bull market describes a sustained period of rising prices and general optimism; a bear market describes a sustained period of falling prices and pessimism, conventionally defined as a drop of 20% or more from a recent high. These terms describe the market's overall mood over months or years, not a single day's move.
