What Is Trading

Trading is the practice of buying and selling financial instruments with the primary goal of profiting from price changes over a relatively short holding period — as opposed to holding an asset for years to benefit from its long-term growth.

Defining trading

Every trade has the same underlying structure: a trader forms a view on where a price is heading, opens a position that profits if that view is correct, and closes it once the target is hit, the view is proven wrong, or a predetermined time has passed. What makes it "trading" rather than "investing" is the time horizon and the reasoning behind it — a trader is usually reacting to price behavior, news, or short-term supply and demand, not to a company's ten-year growth prospects.

How a trade actually happens

Building on the market mechanics covered in Introduction to Financial Markets: a trader places an order through a broker, that order reaches the exchange, and it's matched against an opposing order at an agreed price. The Order Types entry covers the different ways that order can be structured — but at its simplest, a trade is just one decision to buy, paired with a later decision to sell.

Long and short positions

A trader who buys first, expecting the price to rise, is "going long." A trader can also profit from a falling price by "going short" — borrowing shares, selling them immediately, and buying them back later at a lower price to return what was borrowed, pocketing the difference. Short selling carries distinct risks (a rising price produces, in theory, unlimited loss) and specific rules depending on the market, which later entries cover in detail.

What separates trading from gambling

The comparison to gambling comes up often, and it's worth addressing directly. A single trade, viewed in isolation, does resemble a bet: an uncertain outcome, capital at risk, a binary-feeling result. What separates a trading practice from gambling is process — a trader who defines risk before entering a position, sizes that position deliberately, and applies the same method across many trades is playing a game of probabilities where a real, learnable edge can exist over time. Someone placing trades on impulse, without a plan or risk control, is functionally gambling regardless of what asset they're trading. The Risk Management and Trading Psychology sections go into this distinction in depth.

A first look at trading styles

Not all trading happens on the same timeframe. Intraday trading opens and closes positions within a single day; swing trading holds for days to weeks; positional trading holds for weeks to months. Each style suits a different amount of available time, capital, and temperament — the Trading Styles section walks through how to choose.
